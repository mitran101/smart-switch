RIIO-3 & TNUoS: The ¬£108 Network Cost Addition Coming to Your Bill

From April 2026, Ofgem's new price controls unlock ¬£28bn in network investment‚Äîbut transmission charges will nearly double for many customers. Here's what's really happening to your electricity bill.

Overview

From 1 April 2026, the UK enters a new five-year regulatory period for energy network operators known as RIIO-3 (Revenue = Incentives + Innovation + Outputs). This price control framework will determine how much network companies can charge customers to operate, maintain, and expand Britain's electricity and gas infrastructure until March 2031.

Ofgem has approved ¬£28.1 billion in initial investment‚Äî¬£10.3bn for electricity transmission and ¬£17.8bn for gas networks. However, this could rise to ¬£90 billion by 2031 once all planned infrastructure upgrades are accounted for, with electricity transmission alone potentially requiring over ¬£70bn in investment.

The headline impact: Network charges will add ¬£108 to typical household bills by 2031‚Äî¬£60 for electricity and ¬£48 for gas. Transmission Network Use of System (TNUoS) charges, which fund the high-voltage grid, are forecast to nearly double from April 2026.

What is RIIO-3?

RIIO-3 is Ofgem's regulatory framework that sets the allowed revenues for monopoly network operators over 2026-2031. It covers three sectors:

  • Electricity Transmission (ET): The high-voltage network carrying power across Great Britain, operated by National Grid, Scottish Power Transmission, and Scottish Hydro Electric Transmission
  • Gas Transmission (GT): The high-pressure gas network operated by National Gas
  • Gas Distribution (GD): Local gas networks operated by Cadent, Northern Gas Networks, SGN, and Wales & West Utilities

A separate price control, RIIO-ED3, will cover electricity distribution networks from 2028-2033.

Why Network Costs Are Surging

The investment surge addresses three critical infrastructure challenges:

1. Renewable energy integration ‚Äî Offshore wind farms in Scotland and the North Sea generate power far from population centres in southern England. Connecting these renewable sources requires massive transmission infrastructure: new pylons, substations, and high-voltage cables to transport electricity hundreds of miles south.

2. Grid capacity constraints ‚Äî Britain's transmission network was designed for a different energy system. As renewable generation grows and industrial demand for electricity increases, the grid needs significant capacity upgrades to avoid bottlenecks and constraint costs‚Äîwhere the grid pays generators to reduce output or fires up expensive local generation because transmission capacity can't deliver cheaper power from elsewhere.

3. Ageing infrastructure ‚Äî Gas and electricity networks require ongoing maintenance and asset replacement. Metal gas pipes are being replaced with safer plastic alternatives that can also transport hydrogen. Transmission towers and cables are reaching the end of their operational lives and must be upgraded or replaced.

Ofgem argues this investment will deliver long-term benefits: by 2031, grid expansion should reduce wholesale electricity prices and constraint costs by around ¬£80, partially offsetting the ¬£108 in network charges. The net increase to bills is therefore expected to be around ¬£30 per year.

Understanding TNUoS: The Transmission Charge

Transmission Network Use of System (TNUoS) charges fund the operation, maintenance, and expansion of the high-voltage electricity network. These charges appear on electricity bills‚Äîoften buried within the standing charge‚Äîand are set annually by the National Energy System Operator (NESO).

TNUoS has two main components:

  • Fixed residual charges: A flat ¬£/site/day rate based on your connection voltage and capacity. This cannot be avoided by reducing consumption‚Äîit's charged regardless of how much electricity you use.
  • Locational demand charges: Variable charges based on where in Britain you're located and when you use electricity (half-hourly settled demand pays based on consumption during Triad periods‚Äîthe three half-hours of highest system demand each winter).

Households: The ¬£93/year TNUoS cost translates to approximately 26p per day added to electricity standing charges. This hits all customers equally regardless of consumption‚Äîmeaning low users pay proportionally more as a percentage of their total bill.

Businesses: The impact varies dramatically by size and connection type. TNUoS residual charges are banded by connection voltage and agreed capacity. Examples of annual increases by 2030/31:

  • Small businesses (holiday lets, kiosks): ~¬£70/year increase

Critical for fixed contract customers: Most energy supply contracts treat TNUoS as a "pass-through cost." This means even if you have a fixed-rate contract, your supplier can increase standing charges mid-contract when TNUoS rates rise in April 2026. Check your contract terms carefully.

The Bigger Picture: Long-Term Network Investment

RIIO-3 represents the start of a decades-long infrastructure buildout. Electricity transmission alone may require ¬£70bn+ in total investment during 2026-2031, with final spending dependent on project approvals and system needs.

Key investment areas include:

  • Offshore transmission: Connecting new North Sea and Scottish offshore wind farms to the grid
  • Onshore reinforcement: Upgrading transmission corridors from Scotland to England, including controversial pylon projects
  • Substations: Building capacity to handle renewable intermittency and bidirectional power flows
  • Hydrogen-ready gas networks: Replacing metal pipes with plastic infrastructure capable of transporting hydrogen alongside natural gas

Affordability vs Investment: The Trade-Off

RIIO-3 exposes a fundamental tension in energy policy: the UK needs massive grid investment to meet net-zero targets and ensure energy security, but front-loading this investment adds significant near-term costs to bills at a time when households are already struggling with elevated energy prices.

Ofgem has attempted to smooth the bill impact through revenue reprofiling‚Äîreducing allowed revenues in 2026/27 and 2027/28, then increasing them in later years. This means standing charge increases in April 2026 will be somewhat moderated, with steeper rises deferred until 2028-2031.

However, for the average household already paying ~¬£1,750/year for dual fuel, an additional ¬£108 by 2031 represents a 6% increase from network costs alone‚Äîon top of any changes in wholesale energy prices, policy costs, and supplier margins.

What About the Promised Savings?

Ofgem's case for RIIO-3 rests on long-term benefits offsetting near-term costs:

  • Lower constraint costs: By expanding transmission capacity, the grid can deliver cheaper renewable power from Scotland/offshore to demand centres, reducing expensive constraint payments (where generators are paid to switch off or local expensive generation is used instead)
  • Reduced reliance on gas imports: Better renewable integration should reduce exposure to volatile international gas prices
  • Industrial competitiveness: Energy-intensive industries could see bills 10-15% lower than without RIIO-3 investment, thanks to lower wholesale prices and government's "supercharger" mechanism

The timeline matters: investment costs hit bills immediately, while benefits emerge gradually over 5-10 years as infrastructure comes online and system efficiency improves.

Fixed Tariffs and Standing Charge Transparency

The TNUoS increases create particular challenges for the retail energy market:

Pass-through clauses: Most fixed-rate contracts explicitly allow suppliers to pass through changes in network charges. Look for terms like "non-commodity costs," "distribution and transmission charges," or direct references to TNUoS in your contract. These provisions mean your "fixed" rate isn't truly fixed‚Äîstanding charges can rise mid-contract.

Standing charge volatility: TNUoS increases will be delivered primarily through standing charges rather than unit rates. This means households cannot reduce exposure through energy efficiency or consumption reduction‚Äîthe cost is unavoidable.

When comparing tariffs in 2026, pay close attention to standing charge levels. A tariff with a slightly higher unit rate but lower standing charge may be better value if you're a low-to-medium consumer, especially as standing charges continue rising through RIIO-3.

The Distribution Network Question

RIIO-3 covers transmission and gas distribution, but electricity distribution networks (the local "poles and wires" delivering power to homes and businesses) will enter their own price control period, RIIO-ED3, in April 2028.

Distribution network costs already account for a larger portion of electricity bills than transmission costs. If RIIO-ED3 follows a similar pattern of significant investment allowances, households could face further standing charge increases from 2028 onwards.

Business Implications: Planning Ahead

For businesses, particularly those with multiple sites or high electricity demand, the TNUoS changes warrant immediate attention:

  • Review capacity settings: If your kVA capacity is set higher than necessary, you're in a higher charging band and overpaying. Many businesses waste ¬£3,000-¬£10,000 annually on excess capacity
  • Check contract terms: Understand whether your contract fixes TNUoS or allows pass-through. If renewing before April 2026, seek clarity on how 2026/27 increases will be handled
  • Model exposure: Use NESO's published forecasts to calculate your site-specific cost increases and budget accordingly
  • Explore flexibility: Battery storage and demand response can help offset higher network costs by shifting consumption away from peak periods and earning revenue through grid services
