Overview

From 1 January 2026, a brand-new cost called the Nuclear Regulated Asset Base (nRAB) allowance will be added to the Ofgem price cap. This charge funds the construction of the Sizewell C nuclear power station under a financing model that allows developers to recover some costs while the plant is still being built.

Ofgem first consulted on how to add these costs into the cap back in August 2025, and took its final decision on 21 November 2025 ‚Äî but most households will not have followed that process closely. As we now get closer to January 2026, it is a good time to remind people that this new nuclear-related cost is about to be built into their bills.

The allowance will apply to all households on Standard Variable Tariffs (SVTs), and suppliers are expected to reflect it in fixed deals too. For the first time, part of your electricity bill will go toward building a nuclear plant before it generates any power.

Why It's Being Introduced

The Government decided in July 2025 to finance Sizewell C using the nRAB model ‚Äî where consumers contribute small amounts during construction. This lowers long-term financing costs, reduces investor risk, and brings nuclear investment forward sooner.

The Nuclear Energy (Financing) Act 2022 put this framework into law, and after the Government confirmed Sizewell C's allowed revenue for its first charging year in October 2025, Ofgem was required to add the charge into the price cap so suppliers could recover the nuclear levy they must pay.

Because suppliers begin paying nuclear levy costs in December 2025, Ofgem needed to adjust the cap so they could recover these costs fairly ‚Äî without overcharging customers.

How the cost is recovered

  • The allowance is updated every six months within Annex 4 of the price cap, alongside other policy costs like Renewables Obligation, FiTs, ECO and GBIS

Why the first quarter costs more

Suppliers argued that spreading December 2025's levy costs over 12 months would put pressure on their cash flow and create risk if customers switched away before costs were recovered. Several respondents asked for a shorter recovery window.

Ofgem ultimately agreed to a three-month recovery period, meaning all December 2025 nRAB-related levy and operational costs are recouped during Q1 2026, raising the short-term figure from around ¬£13 to ¬£14 for that first period.

What This Means for Your Bill

From January 2026, most suppliers will incorporate the RAB allowance into both SVT and fixed tariffs. The increase is relatively small ‚Äî around ¬£13‚Äì¬£14 per year for a typical household ‚Äî but it is now a standing feature of electricity pricing for as long as the Sizewell C construction-phase RAB charges apply.

This sits alongside other policy costs already built into the cap (such as Contracts for Difference and energy efficiency schemes). Taken together, these small allowances add up and form a visible part of why bills don't just track wholesale prices.

The positive side is that competitive pricing from newer suppliers ‚Äî particularly those offering aggressively priced fixed deals ‚Äî is still helping to offset some of this upward pressure. Even with the nRAB allowance added, there are good-value fixed tariffs available.
