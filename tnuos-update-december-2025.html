TNUoS Charges: December Draft Shows ~20% Reduction vs September Forecast

Following Ofgem's RIIO-3 Final Determinations, transmission network charges will still rise significantly‚Äîbut less than feared. Here's what changed.

Update January 2026 ‚Ä¢ 3 min read

What's Changed

When NESO published its five-year TNUoS forecast in September 2025, the projections showed transmission charges nearly doubling for many customer types from April 2026. However, Ofgem's RIIO-3 Final Determinations, published in December 2025, provided lower allowed revenues for transmission operators than the Draft Determinations suggested.

This resulted in NESO's December draft tariffs coming in approximately 20% lower than the September forecasts, though increases remain substantial compared to current 2025/26 rates.

What This Means for Different Customer Types

Distribution-connected customers are now seeing average fixed TNUoS cost increases of around 64% (vs the 94% originally forecast in September), though this varies significantly by voltage band:

  • Low Voltage Band 3 (LV3): ~41% increase (down from ~70%)
  • Extra High Voltage Band 1 (EHV1): ~104% increase (down from ~141%)

Transmission-connected sites see even greater variation, ranging from 29% increases for some bands to 118% for others‚Äîstill substantial, but more moderate than September's projections suggested.

Important caveat: These December figures are draft tariffs. NESO will publish final tariffs by 31 January 2026, which may differ slightly. However, the direction is clear‚Äîincreases will be material but less severe than initially feared.

Why the Reduction?

Ofgem's Final Determinations for RIIO-3 approved ¬£28.1 billion in baseline investment‚Äîsignificantly lower than what transmission operators initially requested in their business plans. Ofgem cut baseline expenditure by around 15% from the Draft Determinations (which had already proposed a 25% reduction from what companies requested).

Key factors in the reduction:

  • Lower allowed returns: Ofgem set cost of equity at 6.1% (real, at 60% gearing) for transmission‚Äîmore moderate than companies sought
  • Efficiency challenges: Stricter scrutiny of proposed spending, requiring operators to demonstrate efficiency improvements as they scale up investment
  • Revenue smoothing: Ofgem is "reprofiling" allowed revenues‚Äîreducing 2026/27 and 2027/28 charges, then increasing them in later years to smooth bill impacts

What Hasn't Changed

Despite the reduction, the fundamental drivers behind rising TNUoS costs remain:

  • Massive grid reinforcement needed to connect offshore wind and Scottish renewables
  • Aging transmission infrastructure requiring replacement and upgrades
  • Capacity expansion to support electrification and industrial demand growth
  • Regulatory framework (RIIO-3) running from April 2026 to March 2031

The 64% average increase (for distribution-connected customers) still represents the largest single-year jump in transmission charges most businesses will have experienced, and charges are projected to continue rising through 2030/31.

Next Steps and Timeline

  • 31 January 2026: NESO publishes final TNUoS tariffs for 2026/27
  • 1 April 2026: New charges take effect
  • Ongoing: Monitor contract terms for TNUoS pass-through clauses; review site capacity settings; budget for continued increases through 2030/31

Action for businesses: Even with the reduction, now is the time to review your electricity contracts, validate your capacity banding, and model your exposure through to 2031. The increases are still material‚Äîjust less extreme than September's forecast suggested.

The Bigger Picture

While the ~20% reduction is welcome news, it's important to maintain perspective. Households will still see standing charges increase by roughly 48% (¬£42 to ¬£62), and businesses face increases ranging from 41% to over 100% depending on their connection type and voltage band.

This remains a fundamental shift in how transmission costs are recovered, and the upward trajectory will continue through the RIIO-3 period as major infrastructure projects progress. The "good news" is relative‚Äîcharges are rising less steeply than feared, but they're still rising significantly.
