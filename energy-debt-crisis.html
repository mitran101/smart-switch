The Silent Crisis: UK Energy Debt Triples Since 2021

As the price cap yo-yos, millions are trapped in a cycle of unmanageable debt

January 2026 ‚Ä¢ 5 min read

Overview

While politicians celebrate marginal price cap reductions, a different story is unfolding in millions of UK homes. Energy debt has exploded to ¬£4.43 billion ‚Äî more than triple the ¬£1.45 billion owed before the energy crisis began. Over a million households now owe money without any repayment plan in place.

The numbers tell a stark story. Between Q4 2020 and Q2 2025, energy debt has increased by 205%, even as the price cap has fluctuated wildly. But it's not just the total that's alarming ‚Äî it's how quickly things are deteriorating. In the first six months of 2025 alone, over ¬£580 million in new debt was added.

Why Debt Keeps Rising Despite Lower Prices

The disconnect between falling price caps and rising debt isn't a paradox ‚Äî it's a lag effect combined with structural affordability issues. When prices spiked, many households couldn't keep up with payments. They fell behind. Now, even with prices moderating, they're still trying to catch up on old debt while simultaneously paying current bills.

Consider the arithmetic. The average household in arrears owes ¬£1,568 for electricity and ¬£1,324 for gas ‚Äî that's ¬£2,892 total for dual-fuel customers. With the current price cap at around ¬£1,800 per year, these households are essentially trying to pay for two years of energy in one.

The Hidden Cost for Everyone: Energy debt doesn't just affect those who owe it. When customers can't pay, suppliers spread the cost across all billpayers. Current estimates suggest this adds up to ¬£145 per year to the average household bill.

In effect, customers who pay on time are subsidizing the debt of those who can't ‚Äî a system that feels increasingly unfair as living costs remain high for everyone.

The Timeline of Crisis

Looking at the quarterly progression reveals how quickly the situation deteriorated:

  • Q4 2020: ¬£1.45bn in debt ‚Äî the pre-crisis baseline
  • Q1 2022: ¬£1.81bn ‚Äî early warning signs as wholesale prices began rising
  • Q2 2024: ¬£3.69bn ‚Äî debt had already more than doubled
  • Q4 2024: ¬£3.85bn ‚Äî despite government support measures
  • Q2 2025: ¬£4.43bn ‚Äî the current record high

The price cap tells its own story. From a relatively stable ¬£1,138 in Q4 2021, it exploded to ¬£3,549 by Q1 2023 under the Energy Price Guarantee. Even with subsequent reductions, it now sits at ¬£1,837 (Q1 2026) ‚Äî still 61% higher than pre-crisis levels.

Who's Being Hit Hardest?

The debt isn't spread evenly across society. Nearly one-in-five families in the poorest income quintile are behind on at least one priority bill. For many, energy debt represents an impossible choice between heating and eating ‚Äî a phrase that's become depressingly common but no less accurate.

The Resolution Foundation found that energy debt is most concentrated among working-age families with children, particularly those on low-to-middle incomes who earn too much to qualify for benefits but not enough to absorb the shock of doubled energy costs.

The Prepayment Meter Problem: When households fall behind, suppliers often move them to prepayment meters. While this prevents further arrears, it creates a new problem: these households pay for energy at higher rates while also repaying old debt through their top-ups.

Ofgem reports that 1.7 million people were disconnected from electricity at least once a month in 2023 ‚Äî often because they simply ran out of credit on their prepayment meters.

What Happens Next?

Ofgem has announced plans to write off up to ¬£500 million in debt for nearly 200,000 vulnerable households ‚Äî those owing over ¬£100 accrued between April 2022 and March 2024. It's a start, but it will be funded by adding approximately ¬£5 to all energy bills from 2027.

The End Fuel Poverty Coalition is calling for more comprehensive reform, including a debt relief scheme with automatic eligibility for households on means-tested benefits, an end to punitive late fees, and guaranteed protections for prepayment meter customers.

But the fundamental problem remains: even with the price cap at current levels, energy is simply unaffordable for millions. The price cap may be ¬£1,800 per year, but for households earning minimum wage or living on benefits, that's still an enormous burden.

The Bigger Picture

Energy debt is a symptom of a broader cost-of-living crisis. Food insecurity rose from 4.7 million people in 2021-22 to 7.2 million in 2022-23. Council tax arrears have increased by almost half since 2019. The UK's dependency on fossil fuels has cost households an extra ¬£59 billion in energy costs alone since 2021.

While the upcoming Budget measures promise to reduce policy costs by around ¬£150 per household (primarily by moving the Renewables Obligation from bills to general taxation), this offers little comfort to the million-plus households already drowning in debt.

The data is clear: we're not recovering from the energy crisis. For millions of households, we're still in it. Until energy becomes genuinely affordable ‚Äî not just marginally less expensive ‚Äî the debt mountain will continue to grow.
