Q2-26 Ofgem Price Cap: Budget Intervention ‚Äî What's Really Changing

The government claims ¬£150 off your bill. Here's the reality behind the headline, and why the savings might not feel as big as promised.

Overview

The Autumn Budget 2025 introduces the most significant policy cost changes to household energy bills in years. From 1 April 2026, the government will remove two major levies from your bill: the Energy Company Obligation (ECO) ends entirely, and 75% of the Renewables Obligation (RO) shifts to general taxation.

The government is quoting a ¬£150 average reduction ‚Äî but the reality is more nuanced. For a 'typical' dual-fuel medium household as defined by Ofgem, the impact is equivalent to ¬£134 off the price cap.

This won't appear in the Q1-26 price cap (January‚ÄìMarch 2026). Ofgem will confirm the price cap for the period April to June 2026 on 25 February 2026 ‚Äî that's when you'll see these savings reflected.

Why It's Changing

The budget intervention addresses two legacy policy costs that have been quietly inflating electricity bills for years:

Renewables Obligation (RO) ‚Äî The RO scheme was introduced at a time when renewable electricity was significantly more expensive and wholesale prices were low. It requires suppliers to source a proportion of their electricity from renewable generators, funded entirely through your electricity bill. The scheme's value is forecast to total over ¬£8.5bn a year in 2026/27.

From April 2026, 75% of the domestic share of the RO scheme will be funded by the Treasury for three years. This cuts about ¬£88 off bills ‚Äî and all the reduction will go to electricity bills.

Energy Company Obligation (ECO) ‚Äî ECO is a major social programme that since 2013 has spent over ¬£13 billion to upgrade almost 2.6 million homes. It funds insulation and heating improvements for fuel-poor households. ECO currently adds around ¬£60 to the typical household energy bill.

The Government has confirmed that it will end the ECO scheme in March 2026, following criticism and a National Audit Office report which uncovered serious failings in ECO and the linked Great British Insulation Scheme.

Impact on the Q2-26 Price Cap

Without these budget measures, Q2-26 was shaping up badly. External forecasts had been expecting around a ¬£60-¬£80 increase for the Q2 2026 price cap vs the Q1 level, driven by new levies and increased transmission costs.

Now? If the status quo was for a ¬£60-¬£80 price cap increase for Q2 2026, it now looks more like a ¬£55-¬£75 reduction.

But don't expect a dramatically lower bill. Domestic charges for transmission network costs are predicted to increase from ¬£51 to ¬£93 per year ‚Äî enough to take the price cap level over ¬£1,800 for the first time in a year from April 2026.

The budget intervention essentially offsets rising network and other policy costs rather than delivering a genuine reduction from current levels. The two measures help correct a deteriorating trend rather than meaningfully changing the status quo.

The savings are temporary: This funding is set to end in March 2029, when these costs are expected to be added back to consumer electricity bills. Conveniently, that's just before the next general election.

ECO leaves a gap: The decision to abolish ECO is far more contentious. ECO has been the government's main programme for upgrading fuel poor homes for many years and abolishing it will take away ¬£1.7 billion per year from this work.

The government has added ¬£1.5 billion over 3 years (averaging ¬£500 million per year) to the Warm Homes Plan to compensate for the loss of the ECO levy. That's a significant reduction in energy efficiency funding.

Businesses see nothing: Those legacy costs only applied to domestic consumers. Businesses see zero benefit from these measures.

Heat Pump Economics: The Ratio Shift

What This Means for Your Bill

SVT customers will see the ¬£134 reduction reflected from April 2026 ‚Äî but rising network costs will eat into much of it.

Fixed tariff customers should also benefit. The government expects these savings to be passed on to customers on fixed tariffs from April 2026. Martin Lewis has pushed for this to be guaranteed ‚Äî check whether your supplier has committed to passing on the savings.

Northern Ireland exclusion: The ECO scheme doesn't apply to households in Northern Ireland, so the ECO portion of savings won't apply there.
