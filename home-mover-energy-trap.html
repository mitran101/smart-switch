What Happens When You Move: The "Deemed Contract" Trap

Here's what actually happens from an energy supply perspective when you move into a property:

Day 1: Moving In

You take ownership or start your tenancy. The property already has an energy supplier ‚Äì whoever the previous occupant used.

Immediately

You're automatically placed on a "deemed contract" with that supplier. This is their default or standard variable tariff, which is almost always more expensive than deals they actively market to new customers.

First Bill

You receive your first bill based on SVT rates ‚Äì the price cap maximum. Most people simply pay it without realising they could be on a cheaper fixed deal.

Months Later

The intention to "sort out energy" remains on the mental to-do list, but never quite makes it to the top. Meanwhile, you're missing out on savings of ¬£200-250 annually.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Deemed Contracts Cost More

When you move in without actively choosing a supplier, you're placed on a "deemed contract." Since 2019, these have been capped at the same level as Standard Variable Tariffs (SVT) ‚Äì currently ¬£1,758/year for typical usage (January 2026).

While this cap prevents extreme overcharging, you're still paying significantly more than you need to. Competitive fixed deals are typically ¬£200-250/year cheaper than the cap. The difference? You have to actively choose them ‚Äì and most home movers never do.

The New Build Scenario: An Even Bigger Problem

If you're buying a newly built property, the situation is often worse. Developers typically arrange bulk energy supply contracts with specific suppliers during the construction phase. Common suppliers for new builds include British Gas, E.ON, Scottish Hydro (now part of OVO), and others who negotiate developer contracts.

Why New Build Energy Setups Are Problematic

The Developer's Perspective

Developers choose suppliers based on what's cheapest and easiest for them during construction. They typically opt for whoever offers:

  • Lowest upfront connection costs
  • Simplest bulk contracting terms
  • Fastest connection timeline

The developer has zero incentive to secure good ongoing rates for future homeowners ‚Äì they won't be paying the bills.

Your Situation

You inherit whatever arrangement the developer made, which means:

  • No competitive rate negotiation
  • Automatic deemed contract from day one
  • Possible complications with independent gas transporters (IGTs)

You're starting from a position of maximum expense with minimum choice.

The Independent Gas Transporter (IGT) Complication

Many new build developments use Independent Gas Transporters rather than the National Grid to connect properties to the gas network. While this reduces upfront costs for developers, it creates ongoing problems for homeowners:

  • Higher standing charges: IGTs recoup their lower upfront costs through permanently higher standing charges ‚Äì sometimes 50-100% more than National Grid-connected properties
  • Switching difficulties: Not all suppliers work with all IGTs, which can limit your switching options
  • Database delays: New postcodes and meter reference numbers may not be recognised by switching services for weeks or months
  • Limited supplier choice: Only British Gas and SSE (now part of OVO) don't pass IGT surcharges directly to customers, making them disproportionately attractive for new build properties

Over a typical 5-year fixed mortgage period, that's ¬£1,600 in unnecessary energy costs.

Why Don't People Switch?

Despite the clear financial incentive, home mover switching rates remain stubbornly low. Research and industry experience point to several key barriers:

1. Cognitive Overload

Moving house involves coordinating dozens of tasks across multiple weeks. According to Citizens Advice guidance, home movers need to update their address with at least 15-20 different organisations. Energy switching, despite its financial importance, simply gets lost in the chaos.

2. Lack of Immediate Urgency

Unlike cancelling your old council tax or setting up new internet (which have hard deadlines), energy just "works" when you move in. There's no immediate penalty for inaction ‚Äì the overpayment accumulates silently through slightly higher monthly direct debits.

3. Perceived Complexity

Many people assume switching energy suppliers is complicated or risky. Concerns about supply interruption (which cannot actually happen during a legitimate switch) or administrative hassle deter people from taking action.

4. Information Asymmetry

New homeowners, particularly first-time buyers, often don't realise they're on a deemed contract. The supplier sends bills with no indication that these are premium rates. Without historical data for the property or an understanding of typical costs, it's difficult to recognise you're overpaying.

5. The "Later" Trap

Perhaps the biggest factor: the intention to switch "once we're settled" becomes a permanent state of postponement. After a few months, the energy setup feels established, and the inertia becomes even harder to overcome.

What You Should Do When Moving House

‚úÖ The Optimal Timeline

Before completion/move-in: Begin researching who supplies the property and what switching options exist. You cannot physically switch until you legally own or rent the property, but preparation saves time.

Day 1: Take meter readings immediately. Note the current supplier details. Submit these readings to the incumbent supplier.

Week 1: Contact the existing supplier to formally register as the new account holder. Request their best available tariff (not the deemed rate). Simultaneously compare alternative suppliers.

Week 2-3: Initiate a switch to your chosen supplier if a better deal exists. The new supplier manages the entire process.

1Identify Your Supplier

If the previous occupant didn't tell you who supplies the property:

  • For electricity: Use the Energy Networks Association postcode search to find your Distribution Network Operator (DNO), who can tell you your supplier
  • For gas: Use Find My Supplier service with your postcode
  • New builds: Ask your developer's sales team directly ‚Äì they'll have supplier details from the construction contracts

2Register as the New Account Holder

Call the supplier with:

  • Your move-in date (precise date you became legally responsible)
  • Meter readings from day 1
  • Your contact and payment details

Critical: Ask specifically for their "best available tariff" or their standard variable tariff rates. You are not required to accept the deemed contract terms indefinitely ‚Äì Ofgem rules require suppliers to offer you access to their standard tariffs.

3Compare and Switch

Don't assume the inherited supplier is your best option. Use comparison tools to check market rates:

  • SwitchPilot Tariff Tracker ‚Äì Compare current market rates across major suppliers
  • Official price comparison services ‚Äì Accredited by Ofgem
  • Consider fixed-rate deals if you want price certainty, especially if the price cap is expected to rise

4Handle Special Circumstances

Prepayment meters: If your new property has a prepayment meter, contact the supplier before topping up. Previous occupants may have left debt on the meter, which you'd otherwise inadvertently pay off.

New builds with IGT gas: Check if you're on an independent gas transporter network. If so, prioritise British Gas or SSE/OVO to avoid IGT surcharges, or factor these into your comparison calculations.

Smart meters: Verify whether the smart meter is in "smart mode" (communicating with the supplier). First-generation (SMETS1) meters may lose smart functionality when you switch suppliers.

The Switching Process: What Actually Happens

One major barrier to switching is unfounded concern about the process. Here's what genuinely occurs:

  1. You choose a new supplier and tariff ‚Äì This can be done online in 10-15 minutes
  2. Your new supplier contacts your old supplier ‚Äì You don't need to do this yourself
  3. Old supplier issues final bill ‚Äì Based on final meter reading (you should provide this)
  4. Switch completes ‚Äì Typically 17-21 days, but can be as fast as 5 working days
  5. You receive welcome communication from new supplier ‚Äì Confirming account details and payment setup

‚úÖ What Cannot Happen During a Switch

  • Your energy supply cannot be interrupted
  • You cannot be left without a supplier
  • Your meters do not need to be changed (unless moving to/from prepayment)
  • You do not need to contact your Distribution Network Operator

The entire process is regulated by Ofgem and protected by the Energy Switch Guarantee, which mandates maximum timeframes and automatic compensation if things go wrong.

Special Considerations for Renters

If you're renting rather than buying, you still have the right to switch energy suppliers ‚Äì with some caveats:

‚úÖ You CAN Switch If:

  • Your name is on the energy bill
  • You pay the supplier directly
  • Energy costs are not included in your rent

Your landlord cannot prevent you from switching under these circumstances, even if they have a "preferred supplier" clause in your tenancy agreement. Ofgem rules give you this right.

‚ùå You CANNOT Switch If:

  • The landlord's name is on the bill
  • Energy costs are included in your rent
  • The landlord bills you separately for energy

In these cases, the landlord is the customer of the energy supplier, not you. You pay the landlord, who pays the supplier.

Why This Matters Beyond Individual Savings

The home mover switching problem isn't just about individual household finances ‚Äì it has broader implications for the UK energy market:

Market Efficiency

Low switching rates among home movers reduce competitive pressure on suppliers. If millions of customers remain on default deemed contracts indefinitely, suppliers have less incentive to offer attractive deals to retain customers.

Consumer Detriment

With approximately 1.2 million residential transactions annually and average potential savings of ¬£200-250/year from switching away from SVT, the collective missed savings by UK home movers likely exceeds ¬£250 million annually. This is money that could be spent elsewhere in the economy or saved by households.

New Build Inequity

First-time buyers purchasing new builds face particularly acute disadvantages ‚Äì higher energy costs from day one in properties that should be more efficient. This undermines the government's housing policy goals and makes new builds less financially attractive than their EPC ratings suggest.
